Gameplay

Limited power makes strategy matter.

You do not stand outside the map moving obedient pieces. You sit inside a regime that hesitates, runs short of grain, misreads messages, and gets pushed back by local society.

Seat and Orders

Who Are You, and Who Actually Obeys?

You choose a position of power, not an abstract nation. An emperor, grand secretary, field commander, garrison leader, or staff adviser has different authority, sightlines, and personal risks. The same order can cost very different things from different seats.

What You Navigate

  1. Choose a historical seat with real authority and real blind spots.
  2. Turn intent into direct orders, formal requests, private influence, or intelligence work.
  3. Re-prioritize when messages are late, authority is thin, and local actors resist.

Local Friction

Why Does the Local World Push Back?

Counties, gentry, salt merchants, soldiers, refugees, roads, and granaries are not scenery. They decide whether an arrived order becomes action, delay, bargaining, or a new crisis.

What You Navigate

  1. Read who actually controls a place, and who can provide cooperation.
  2. Divide scarce resources between relief, requisition, route repair, route security, credit, and tax pressure.
  3. Watch morale, refugees, food prices, rumor, and legitimacy pull on each other.

War Pressure

Why Is War More Than Pushing a Front?

Armies can defend, contest crossings, and pressure local society, but they also consume grain and pay, damage order, and create debt. Battles emerge from routes, supply, commanders, morale, and local tolerance.

What You Navigate

  1. Choose what to defend, rescue, abandon, and whether your orders can arrive in time.
  2. Trade off battle risk, supply pressure, local tolerance, and expanding military power.
  3. Deal with occupation, defeat, discipline, and front-line autonomy.

Consequences

How Does the Crisis Keep Rolling Forward?

Recognition, truces, loans, reform, rumor, and historical memory reshape the next round of choices. The game asks not only whether you survived a crisis, but what price moved the situation forward.

What You Navigate

  1. Use diplomacy, recognition, and exchange to buy time instead of solving everything by force.
  2. Push institutional change while carrying enforcement cost and local backlash.
  3. Let earlier choices persist as modifiers, reputation, grudges, and precedents.
